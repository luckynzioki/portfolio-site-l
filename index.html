Measurement theory background

Classical Test Theory (CTT)
Foundation of psychometric analysis · BSc & MSc statistical methods
CTT models an observed score as the sum of a true score and measurement error: X = T + E. The framework underpins reliability estimation (Cronbach's α, split-half, test-retest) and item analysis through difficulty indices and point-biserial correlations. CTT remains widely used due to its simplicity and modest sample requirements, though its item statistics are sample-dependent.
Easy items (p > 0.70)
Moderate items (0.30–0.70)
Hard items (p < 0.30)
🔬 Item difficulty (p-value) and item-total correlation (point-biserial r) are the two core CTT item statistics. The scatter above reveals that most moderate-difficulty items have the highest discriminating power — consistent with measurement theory predictions.
Item Response Theory (IRT)
Modern psychometrics · 2PL and 3PL models · applied in education and survey research
IRT models the probability of a correct response as a function of person ability (θ) and item parameters. The 3-parameter logistic model (3PL) incorporates discrimination (a), difficulty (b), and guessing (c). Unlike CTT, IRT item parameters are sample-independent and person ability estimates are item-independent — enabling adaptive testing, equating, and differential item functioning (DIF) analysis.
High discrimination (a=1.8)
Moderate discrimination (a=1.0)
Low discrimination (a=0.5)
📈 Item Characteristic Curves (ICCs) show the probability of a correct response across the ability continuum. Steeper curves (higher a-parameter) indicate more discriminating items. The inflection point on the curve corresponds to the item's difficulty parameter b.

Key psychometric concepts

Reliability (CTT)
Consistency of measurement. Estimated via Cronbach's α (internal consistency), test-retest correlation, or split-half coefficient. α > 0.70 conventionally acceptable; α > 0.90 for high-stakes decisions.
Test Information Function (IRT)
IRT equivalent of reliability — specifies where on the ability scale a test is most precise. Peaks at the ability level where items are most discriminating. Enables targeted test construction for specific θ ranges.
Differential Item Functioning (DIF)
An item exhibits DIF when examinees of equal ability from different groups have systematically different probabilities of a correct response. Detected via Mantel-Haenszel or IRT likelihood-ratio methods. Critical for test fairness and validity.
Construct Validity
Evidence that a test measures the intended latent construct. Evaluated through factor analysis (convergent & discriminant validity), known-groups comparisons, and structural equation modelling — all applicable under both CTT and IRT frameworks.
Item Difficulty (p-value)
In CTT: proportion of respondents answering correctly. In IRT: the b-parameter (difficulty) is the ability level at which P(correct) = 0.50 (or 0.5+c/2 in 3PL). Optimal test design targets moderate difficulty (0.30–0.70 in CTT).
Point-Biserial Discrimination
CTT measure of item quality — correlation between item score (0/1) and total test score. Values below 0.20 indicate poor discrimination; negative values indicate item miskeys or construct contamination. Analogous to the a-parameter in IRT.
🔭 The Test Information Function (TIF) peaks at θ ≈ 0, indicating the test discriminates best among average-ability examinees. Measurement error (SE = 1/√I(θ)) is lowest at the peak and rises at the extremes — implying that pass/fail decisions near the cut-score are most reliable.
